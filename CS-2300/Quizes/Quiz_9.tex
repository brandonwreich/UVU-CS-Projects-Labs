\subsection*{1.}
Use mathematical induction to prove that for any positive integer $n$,
\[
\sum_{j=1}^{n} j \cdot 2^j = (n-1)2^{n+1} + 2.
\] \\[0.5em]
\textcolor{blue}{\textbf{Proof by Mathematical Induction:}} \\[0.5em]
\textcolor{blue}{\textbf{Base Case} ($n = 1$):} \\[0.3em]
\textcolor{blue}{
The left-hand side is $\displaystyle\sum_{j=1}^{1} j \cdot 2^j = 1 \cdot 2^1 = 2$.
The right-hand side is $(1-1)2^{2} + 2 = 0 + 2 = 2$.
Since both sides equal 2, the base case holds.
} \\[0.5em]
\textcolor{blue}{\textbf{Inductive Hypothesis:}} \\[0.3em]
\textcolor{blue}{
Assume that for some integer $k \geq 1$,
\[
\sum_{j=1}^{k} j \cdot 2^j = (k-1)2^{k+1} + 2.
\]
} \\[0.3em]
\textcolor{blue}{\textbf{Inductive Step:}} \\[0.3em]
\textcolor{blue}{
We must show that $\displaystyle\sum_{j=1}^{k+1} j \cdot 2^j = k \cdot 2^{k+2} + 2$.
\begin{align*}
\sum_{j=1}^{k+1} j \cdot 2^j
&= \sum_{j=1}^{k} j \cdot 2^j + (k+1) \cdot 2^{k+1} \\
&= \bigl[(k-1)2^{k+1} + 2\bigr] + (k+1) \cdot 2^{k+1} \quad \text{(by the inductive hypothesis)} \\
&= \bigl[(k-1) + (k+1)\bigr] \cdot 2^{k+1} + 2 \\
&= 2k \cdot 2^{k+1} + 2 \\
&= k \cdot 2^{k+2} + 2.
\end{align*}
This is exactly $((k+1)-1)2^{(k+1)+1} + 2$, which is what we needed to show.
} \\[0.3em]
\textcolor{blue}{
By the principle of mathematical induction, the statement holds for all positive integers $n$. $\blacksquare$
}

\subsection*{2.}
Consider the following inductive proof that all discrete math students in a class receive the same grade. Is this proof valid? If yes, explain why. If no, point out all places where the proof is incorrect. \\[1em]
\textcolor{blue}{
The proof is \textbf{not valid}. The inductive step breaks down at $k = 1$. When the class has 2 students $\{A, B\}$, removing $A$ leaves $\{B\}$ and removing $B$ leaves $\{A\}$, these two groups share no students in common. So the step from $n = 1$ to $n = 2$ is never actually proven, and the conclusion fails.
} \\[0.3em]
